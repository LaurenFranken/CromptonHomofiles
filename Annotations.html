BIOGRAPHICAL ANNOTATIONS

Baker, Charles Kelley, III (1945–) ("Kelley"). Editor, The Daily Nebraskan. Baker enrolled at the University of Nebraska in 1963 and received a BA in English in 1971. In February 1966, his first Daily Nebraskan byline appeared in an article about Allen Ginsburg's visit to the university. Baker continued to contribute to the newspaper until his graduation, serving as editor for the 1970–71 school year. During the summer of 1972, he wrote for the Lincoln Evening Journal, and in 1974 he received a JD from University of Nebraska College of Law. Baker subsequently practiced law for various firms and associations in Lincoln.

Burrows, William G. (unknown). Professor, Nebraska Psychiatric Institute. Burrows was a professor of psychiatry and an associate director of the Nebraska Psychiatric Institute from 1969 to 1973. The institute was a teaching, research, and intensive treatment hospital and was headquartered at the University of Nebraska Medical Center in Omaha, NE.

Caldwell, Warren W. (1925–). Chairman, anthropology department, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Davenport, IA, Caldwell received degrees from San Mateo California Junior College, Stanford University, and Washington University. He joined the University of Nebraska faculty in 1960 and became the chair of the anthropology department in 1969. Caldwell also served as the curator of anthropology at the University of Nebraska State Museum.

Canon, Harry James (unknown). Director, Counseling Services, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Canon received a BA from Lycoming College in Williamsport, PA, an MA from Pennsylvania State University, and a PhD from the University of Nebraska. In 1966, Canon became the director of Counseling Services at the University of Nebraska.

Carpenter, Terry McGovern (1900–1978) ("Terrible Terry"). State senator, Nebraska Legislature, 1953–1955, 1957–1961, 1963–1974. Born in Cedar Rapids, IA, Carpenter moved to Scottsbluff, NE, in 1916 where he worked for various railroad and coal businesses. In 1932, voters of Nebraska's 5th Congressional District elected him as a Democrat to the US House of Representatives. He served only one term. After unsuccessful campaigns for both the US Senate and the Governor of Nebraska, Carpenter started various businesses in Scottsbluff, including a gasoline refinery. In 1952, he successfully won election to the Nebraska Legislature and served intermittently until 1974 as both a Democrat and a Republican, though the Nebraska Legislature is officially nonpartisan. In 1978, after Carpenter died, the Nebraska Legislature passed Legislative Bill 955, which renamed the Nebraska Educational Telecommunications Building the Terry M. Carpenter Educational Telecommunications Building.

Cole, James Kimbrough (1935–2010) ("Jim"). Professor, psychology department, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Asheville, NC, Cole graduated from Mars Hill College in Mars Hill, NC, in 1955 and received both an MA and a PhD from the University of Kentucky in 1962 and 1965 respectively. In 1964, he joined the faculty of the University of Nebraska, where he led and shaped the Clinical Psychology Training Program, and retired in 1997. Cole received two University of Nebraska–Lincoln recognitions for his work with and defense of the 1970 "Proseminar on Homophile Studies," including the 1989 James A. Lake Award for Academic Freedom and the 2010 University of Nebraska–Lincoln Chancellor's Award for Outstanding Contributions to the Gay, Lesbian, Bisexual, and Transgender Community.

Creason, Joseph Dean (1943–1991) ("JD") ("Joe"). Gay activist; student, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Hastings, NE, Creason enrolled at the University of Nebraska in 1967 as a junior division student in the Teachers College. In October 1970, he and David Patrick McIntosh co-founded the Lincoln Gay Action Group (LINGAG). From 1979 to 1985, Creason was the volunteer services coordinator for the Lincoln Regional Center in Lincoln, NE, and from 1985 to 1988, he was the assistant director of the Alcoholism and Drug Abuse Council of Nebraska. In November 1988, he became the executive director of the Lincoln Council on Alcoholism and Drugs but resigned six months later after receiving a positive AIDS diagnosis. Creason did not test for HIV, the virus that causes AIDS, until after he began showing signs of the disease. Consequently, he spent the last two years of his life speaking publicly about AIDS, and he served on the board of the Nebraska AIDS Project. Creason also served on the Governor’s Council on Volunteerism, the Nebraska Crime Prevention Commission, the Mayor’s Task Force on Illegal Drugs, and the executive board of the Nebraska Organization of Volunteer Leaders.

Crowl, Philip A. (1914–1991). Chairman, history department, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Dayton, OH, Crowl received degrees from Swarthmore College, Yale University Law School, University of Iowa, and Johns Hopkins University. From 1941 to 1942 he taught history at Princeton University but left academia to serve in the US Navy from 1942 to 1945. He subsequently returned to Princeton but from 1949 to 1957, he served as a civilian historian for the Office of the Chief of Military History of the US Army in Washington DC. From 1957 to 1967 he served as an intelligence officer for the US Department of State. Crowl returned to academia as a professor of history and chair of the department of history at the University of Nebraska from 1967 to 1973.

Dobler, William O. (1926–2011) ("Bill"). Newspaper editor. Born in St. Joseph, MO, Dobler moved to Lincoln, NE, in 1948 where he enrolled at the University of Nebraska and began studying journalism. The following year he began reporting part-time for The Lincoln Star, joining the newspaper staff permanently after graduating from the university with a certificate in journalism in 1950. By 1952, Dobler became the city hall reporter, and in 1957, he became the editorial editor and served in varying editorial positions until his retirement in 1989.

Doerr, Lawrence Emil (1931–2000) ("Larry"). Presbyterian minister. Born in Minneapolis, MN, Doerr received a BA in history from Macalester College in St. Paul, MN, in 1953 and an MDiv in theology and history from Union Theological Seminary in New York, NY, in 1956. As an ordained Presybeterian minister, Doerr pastored a parish in southern Minnesota for four years before he entered United Campus Christian Fellowship, a campus ministries organization at the University of Minnesota in 1960. In 1970, he moved to Lincoln, NE, where he became the director of United Ministries in Higher Education, a campus ministries organization at the University of Nebraska–Lincoln, and he immediately began supporting students involved in both Vietnam War and gay rights protests. Doerr served as director until his retirement in 1996.

Dressel, David Allan (1942–). Lutheran minister. Born in Rice, MN, Dressel received a MDiv in theology from Union Seminary in Richmond, VA, in 1969. He was ordained the same year and then moved to Lincoln, NE, where he pastored at the University Lutheran Church until 1972. From 1972 to 1980, he pastored at the Wittenberg Chapel in Grand Forks, ND. He thereafter moved to East Lansing, MI, where he pastored at the Martin Luther Chapel.

Eisenhower, Dwight David (1890–1969) ("Ike"). President, United States, 1953–1961.


Greenberg, Benjamin Norton (1903–1988) ("Ben"). Regent, University of Nebraska, 1953–1971. Born in Omaha, NE, Norton received a BA from the University of Nebraska in 1925 and an MD from the University of Nebraska College of Medicine in 1928. By 1935, he moved to York, NE, where he practiced medicine, specializing in the eye, ear, and throat, until his retirement in 1988. From 1953 to 1971, Greenberg served on the University of Nebraska Board of Regents.

Haywood, Charles (1950–2000) (“Chuck”). Director, University of Nebraska–Lincoln Psychological Clinic. Born in Brooklyn, NY, Haywood received a BA from Fordham University in the Bronx, NY, and a PhD in clinical psychology from the University of Texas at Austin. He served as the director of the University of Nebraska–Lincoln Psychological Clinic before moving to Buffalo, NY, to serve as the executive director of Crisis Services of Buffalo. From 1986 to 1998, Haywood served as a psychologist for the State of New York as well as for the US Department of Veteran Affairs. He consulted people and families in crisis on intervention techniques as well as volunteering as a counselor of AIDS patients.

Herman, Richard Lyonel (1920–2015) ("Dick"). Regent, University of Nebraska, 1967–1971. Born in Fremont, NE, Herman attended the University of Washington and the University of Nebraska. In 1952, Herman became president of Herman Brothers, Inc., a hazardous waste collection and transportation company. In 1970, Herman resigned from the Board of Regents after the Nebraska Republican Party elected him to be its national committeeman.

Hitler, Adolf (1889–1945). Chancellor/Führer, Germany, 1933–1945.


Hobson, Merk (1921–1977). Acting chancellor/vice chancellor, University of Nebraska. Born in Little Norway, WI, Hobson received a BS from the University of Wisconsin in 1943 and both an MS and a PhD from Northwestern University in 1948 and 1951 respectively. In 1950, he joined the faculty of the College of Engineering and Architecture at the University of Nebraska, becoming the college's dean in 1957. In 1965, Hobson became vice chancellor for research and dean of the Graduate College, and in 1966, he became dean of faculties. In 1968, the University of Nebraska reorganized into three separate institutions: the University of Nebraska–Lincoln, the flagship institution; the University of Nebraska Medical Center, the former University of Nebraska College of Medicine; and the University of Nebraska at Omaha, the former University of Omaha. At the time the leader of the university system was known as chancellor and the administrative leaders of each institution were known as presidents. Upon the restructuring, Clifford Hardin become the university system's first chancellor but resigned five months later when President Richard Nixon appointed him to be the US Secretary of Agriculture. Consequently, Hobson served as the university system's acting chancellor, until the Nebraska Board of Regents named Durwood Varner chancellor in early-1970. Upon Varner's arrival, Hobson became vice chancellor of the University of Nebraska. He retired in 1974 and returned to Little Norway to operate a family business.

Holder, Preston (1907–1980). Professor, anthropology department, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Wabash, IN, Holder enrolled at the University of Nebraska in 1926 and contributed to the Prairie Schooner literary magazine. In 1932, he transferred to the University of California, Berkeley, where he received a BA in anthropology in 1935. In 1950, he received a PhD in anthropology from Columbia University, and in 1958, Holder returned to the University of Nebraska and joined the anthropology department. He retired in 1973.

Julian, Joseph (1935–2020). Associate professor, sociology department, University of Nebraska, 1967–Lincoln. Born in San Francisco, CA, Julian received a BA from San Francisco State University and an MA and PhD from the University of Washington. Throughout his career, he taught variously at the University of Washington; Kansas State University; the University of Nebraska; California State University, Bakersfield; and San Francisco State University.

Kruger, James E. (1915–1979). Superintendent, Nebraska State Patrol. Born in Sterling, NE, Kruger entered the Nebraska State Patrol in 1940, serving as deputy superintendent from 1962 to 1967 and as superintendent from 1967 to when he retired in 1973.

Larson, Garnet Ireva (1904–1998). Professor, School of Social Work, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Franklin County, NE, Larson received a BS in education and an MA in English from the University of Nebraska in 1929 and 1930 respectively. In 1935, she received a PhD in English from the University of Kansas and thereafter taught English at Chadron State Teachers College in Chadron, NE, until 1944. In 1946, Larson received an MA in social work from the University of Pittsburgh and worked for the Division of Research and Statistics for the North Dakota Public Welfare Board from 1946 to 1949. In 1949, she joined the faculty of the School of Social Work at the University of Nebraska and retired in 1973.

Leinieks, Val (1932–2019). Professor, chair, classics department, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Liepaja, Latvia, Leinieks moved to the United States in 1949. He received a BA and MA from Cornell University in 1955 and 1956 respectively and a PhD from Princeton University in 1962. He began his career in academia as an assistant professor of classics at Cornell College in Mt. Vernon, IA, and in 1964, he became an associate professor at Ohio State University. In 1966, Leinieks moved to Lincoln, NE, where he served as a professor and the chair of the classics department until his retirement in 1983.

Magrath, Peter C. (1933–). Dean of Faculties, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in New York, NY, Magrath received a BA from the University of New Hampshire and a PhD at Cornell University. He began his career in higher education in 1967 at Brown University, where he served as a professor of political science and later as the associate dean of the graduate school. Magrath served as the dean of faculties for the University of Nebraska–Lincoln from 1969 to 1972. Magrath went on to serve as president of the State University of New York at Binghamton (1972–74); the University of Minnesota (1974–84) and the University of Missouri System (1985–91). In 1992, Magrath assumed the presidency of the National Association of State Universities and Land-Grant Colleges, a position which he held until 2005. In 2006, Magrath became the Senior Advisor to the College Board, and in 2007, he was elected to a three-year term on the Board of Directors of the American Association of Community Colleges.

Martin, Louis Chapman (1928–2007). Psychiatrist, University Student Health Center, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Omaha, NE, Martin received both a BA and an MA in classical languages from St. Louis University in St. Louis, MO, in 1952 and 1954 respectively. He also received an MS in physiology from Creighton University School of Medicine in Omaha, NE, and an MD from the University of Nebraska College of Medicine in 1960 and 1962 respectively. By 1968, Martin was director of mental health services at the University Student Health Center, and in 1973, he moved to Hastings, NE, to direct the Hastings Regional Center. He resigned a few months later and moved to Omaha where he taught at Creighton University and worked for the Douglas County Hospital. In 1985, he accepted a position at the Lincoln Regional Center in Lincoln, NE, and he practiced psychiatry there until 2007 when a patient he was killed by a patient.

McBride, John G. (1926–2008) ("Jack"). Founder, Nebraska Educational Television. Born in Omaha, NE, McBride received a BA from Creighton University in Omaha, NE, in 1948 and an MA from Northwestern University in Evanston, IL, in 1949. In 1953, the University of Nebraska hired McBride as director of television and general manager of KUON-TV, Nebraska's nascent public broadcasting network. He served in this role until 1996, overseeing the development of Nebraska Educational Television in 1963. McBride influenced the architectural design of the Nebraska Educational Telecommunications Building, funding for which state Senator Terry Carpenter secured through Legislative Bill 65 in 1969. In 1971, during the building's construction on the east campus of the University of Nebraska–Lincoln, Carpenter threatened to repurpose the building for use by the university's College of Law through Legislative Resolution 55; however, his motion failed 6–28 on April 30, 1971. The building subsequently opened in 1972, and in 1978, the Nebraska Legislature passed Legislative Bill 955, which renamed it the Terry M. Carpenter Educational Telecommunications Building. In 2011, the Nebraska Legislature passed Legislative Bill 122, which once again renamed it the Terry M. Carpenter and Jack G. McBride Educational Telecommunications Building.

McCashland, Benjamin W. (1921–2001). Assistant Dean, Graduate College, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Geneva, NE, McCashland received a BS, a MS, and a PhD from the University of Nebraska in 1947, 1948, and 1955 respectively. Upon completion of his doctoral work, he continued teaching at the university as assistant professor of physiology until 1965 when he became the assistant dean of the Graduate College. McCashland resigned on August 31, 1970 and moved to Moorhead, MN, where he became the dean of graduate studies at Moorhead State University. He retired in 1983.

McIntosh, David Patrick (1950–1989). Gay activist; student, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Philadelphia, PA, McIntosh attended Creighton Preparatory School in Omaha, NE, and enrolled at the University of Nebraska–Lincoln in 1969 in the College of Arts and Sciences. In October 1970, he and Joseph Dean Creason co-founded the Lincoln Gay Action Group (LINGAG). In May 1972, McIntosh attended George McGovern's town hall meeting in Lincoln where he asked the presidential nominee, "What is your position on gay rights?" McGovern was allegedly dumbfounded and gave a perfunctory response. After graduating from the university in 1973, McIntosh worked with The Play Group, an alternative theater company in Knoxville, TN, and in 1979, he moved to New York, NY, where wrote and worked both in the theater and at the New York City Gay and Lesbian Center where he designed an ornamental garden. He died from complications of AIDS in 1989. His papers are housed in the rare manuscripts collection at Cornell University in Ithaca, NY.

McKinney, Jerome B. (1939–). Instructor, political science department, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. McKinney received a BA and MA from New Mexico State Highlands University in Las Vegas, NM, and a PhD from the University of Missouri. McKinney was an instructor and assistant professor at the University of Nebraska–Lincoln from 1968 to 1970.

Nixon, Richard (1913–1994) ("Dick"). President, United States, 1969–1974.


Osborne, Robert George (1931–2012) ("Bob"). Psychiatrist; deputy director of medical services, Nebraska Department of Public Institutions. Born in Fargo, ND, Osborne graduated from the University of Nebraska College of Medicine in 1956 and completed his residency at the Nebraska Psychiatric Institute in Omaha, NE. From 1957 to 1959, he actively served in the US Navy. In 1962, he began work at the Norfolk State Hospital in Norfolk, NE, becoming its clinical director in 1964. In 1967, Governor Norbert Tiemann named Osborne the deputy director for medical services of the Nebraska Department of Public Institutions. He served in that role until 1972 when he became the director of the Lincoln Regional Center in Lincoln, NE.

Pedersen, Henry F., Jr. (1930–). State senator, Nebraska Legislature, 1965–1971. Born in Omaha, NE, Pedersen received a BS in law from the University of Nebraska and a JD from Creighton University in Omaha, NE, in 1951 and 1953 respectively. From 1962 to 1964, he served as the chairman of the Republican Party in Douglas County, NE, and from 1965 to 1971, he served in the Nebraska Legislature. In 1970, Pedersen unsuccessfully ran for lieutenant governor of Nebraska, and in 1973, he moved to Estes Park, CO.

Prichard, Keith W. (1929–2016). Professor, Teachers College, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Putnam County, IN, Prichard received a BS in education from Indiana University in 1952. He later received an MA from Columbia University and a PhD from Harvard University in 1961 respectively. From 1961 to 1967, he taught as an associate professor of history and philosophy of education at Wake Forest University in Winston-Salem, NC. In 1967, Prichard became an associate professor in the Teachers College at the University of Nebraska. In 1970, university students honored him as professor of the year. Prichard retired in 1995.

Prokop, Robert J. (1934–2020). Regent, University of Nebraska, 1971–1983. Born in Wilber, NE, Prokop received a BS in chemistry and mathematics, an MS in chemistry, and a PhD in philosophy from the University of Nebraska in 1956, 1958, and 1963 respectively. In 1966, he received an MD from the University of Nebraska College of Medicine and went on to practice medicine for thirty-five years. On March 11, 1970, Prokop filed his candidacy for the University of Nebraska Board of Regents and defeated incumbent Dr. Benjamin Norton Greenberg in the November election. He served until 1983.

Roosevelt, Franklin Delano (1882–1945) ("FDR"). President, United States, 1933–1945.


Rudolph, Wallace M. (1930–2017). Professor, College of Law, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Chicago, IL, Rudolph received a BA and a JD from the University of Chicago in 1950 and 1953 respectively. From 1957 to 1958, he instructed at the University of Chicago Law School, and in 1963, he joined the faculty at the University of Nebraska College of Law. In 1976, Rudolph moved to Tacoma, WA, and joined the Puget Sound Law School (now Seattle University Law School), serving as dean until 1980 and as teaching faculty until 1996. In 1997, Rudolph moved to Orlando, FL, to serve as the dean of the University of Orlando Law School (now Dwayne O. Andreas School of Law) but was fired fourteen months later in part due to his failure to secure accreditation for the school.

Schwartzkopf, Edward (1919–2006) ("Ed"). Regent, University of Nebraska, 1967–1985. Born in Lincoln, NE, Schwartzkopf enrolled at the University of Nebraska in 1938 where he became a football star and played as starting guard for the 1941 Rose Bowl team. From 1941 to 1945, Schwartzkopf actively served in the US Army. In 1948, he graduated with a BS in education and went on to have a thirty-four-year career as a teacher and administrator with Lincoln Public Schools. From 1967 to 1985, Schwartzkopf served on the University of Nebraska Board of Regents.

Simpson, Harold Dwaine (1926–2001). State senator, Nebraska Legislature, 1967–1976. Born in Harlan, IA, Simpson served in the US Army Air Corps from 1945 to 1947 and was stationed at the Lincoln Army Airfield in Lincoln, NE, by the end of his service. He remained in Lincoln and began a career working for the Gooch Milling & Elevator Company. From 1967 to 1976, Simpson served in the Nebraska Legislature, and from 1977 to 1989, he served on the Nebraska Public Service Commission.

Soshnik, Joseph (1920–2002) ("Joe"). President, University of Nebraska–Lincoln. Born in Omaha, NE, Soshnik received a BS from Creighton University in Omaha, NE, in 1941, an MS from the University of Denver in Denver, CO, in 1943, and a PhD in business organization and management from the University of Nebraska in 1952. The same year, he became an auditor and budget consultant at Creighton University and served in that role until 1957 when he became the comptroller for the University of Nebraska. In 1962, Soshnik became the university's vice chancellor for business and finance, and in 1967, he became vice chancellor for administration. In 1968, the University of Nebraska reorganized into three separate institutions: the University of Nebraska–Lincoln, the flagship institution; the University of Nebraska Medical Center, the former University of Nebraska College of Medicine; and the University of Nebraska at Omaha, the former University of Omaha. At the time the leader of the university system was known as chancellor and the administrative leaders of each institution were known as presidents. Upon the restructuring, Soshnik became the first (and only) president of the University of Nebraska–Lincoln, essentially the thirteenth chancellor of the Lincoln campuses. He resigned in March 1971, in part due to his handling of a student affair in which Vietnam War protesters occupied the university's ROTC building in May 1970. Following his resignation, Soshnik joined the Omaha-based investment banking firm, Kirkpatrick, Pettis, Smith, Polian, Inc. and served in various executive roles.

Spiers, Duane Edwin (1930–2005). Associate professor, psychiatry department, Creighton University. Born in Denver, CO, Spiers was an associate professor of psychiatry at Creighton University in Omaha, NE, from 1967 to 1977.

Tiemann, Norbert (1924–2012) ("Nobby"). Governor, State of Nebraska, 1967–1971.


Varner, Durward Belmont (1917–1999) ("Woody"). Chancellor/president, University of Nebraska. Born in Cottonwood, TX, Varner received a BS in agricultural economics from the Agricultural and Mechanical College of Texas in College Station, TX, in 1940 and an MA in agricultural economics from the University of Chicago in 1949. Throughout the 1940s and the 1950s, he served in various professorial and administrative roles at Michigan State University in East Lansing, MI, and in 1959, he became the inaugural chancellor of Michigan State University–Oakland in Rochester Hills, MI. In 1968, the University of Nebraska reorganized into three separate institutions: the University of Nebraska–Lincoln, the flagship institution; the University of Nebraska Medical Center, the former University of Nebraska College of Medicine; and the University of Nebraska at Omaha, the former University of Omaha. At the time the leader of the university system was known as chancellor and the administrative leaders of each institution were known as presidents. In 1970, Varner became the second chancellor of the system, a title which was altered to president the following year. He thereafter served as president of the University of Nebraska until 1976 at which point in time he became president of the University of Nebraska Foundation.

ORGANIZATION ANNOTATIONS

American Psychiatric Association ("APA"). Founded in 1844, the American Psychiatric Association was first established as the Association of Medical Superintendents of American Institutions for the Insane. It was the first national medical society in the United States.

Council on Religion and the Homosexual ("CRH"). Founded in 1964, the Council on Religion and the Homosexual was a joint venture of protestant religious leaders and homosexual activists in the San Francisco, CA, area.

Kinsey Institute of Indiana University. Founded in 1947 by Dr. Alfred Kinsey, after whom the organization was later named in 1981, the Institute for Sex Research sought to further research on human sexuality and manage various research materials. The Kinsey Institute has since grown to focus not only on multidisciplinary research in neuroscience, psychology, anthropology, and gender studies, but also on education, outreach, and historical preservation.

Lincoln Gay Action Group ("LINGAG") or University of Nebraska Gay Action Group ("UNGAG"). In October 1970, Joseph Dean Creason and David Patrick McIntosh co-founded the Lincoln Gay Action Group, which held meetings and public forums at the United Ministries in Higher Education at 333 North 14th Street in Lincoln, NE. LINGAG's original mission was "to study the problems of human sexuality as they relate to both women and men and to provide information about these problems to the people of Lincoln." The University of Nebraska officially recognized the organization by fall of 1971, at which time it took on the acronym UNGAG.

Legislative Resolution 49 Committee ("LR 49 Committee") (1969). In 1969, the Nebraska Legislature adopted Legislative Resolution 49, which created a committee to study “Nebraska laws concerning sexual offenders, and the methods used for treatment and incarceration of such persons.” Senators Henry F. Pedersen, Jr. and Terry Carpenter were chair and vice-chair of the committee respectively.

ONE Institute. Founded in 1955, the ONE Institute of Los Angeles, CA, was the first organization in the United States to offer a college-level course on homosexuality from a gay-affirmative perspective.

The Phoenix Society for Individual Freedom. Founded in 1966, the Phoenix Society for Individual Freedom was the first homophile organization in Kansas City, MO. Activists sought to improve the legal, social, and economic status of homosexuals in the community. Drew Shafer spearheaded the movement and eventually acted as the society’s first president. His mother Phyllis served as the secretary of the Phoenix Society, and his father Robert, a printer by trade, enabled Shafer to publish and distribute the Phoenix Society newsletter.

Reserve Officers' Training Corps ("ROTC").


United Ministries in Higher Education ("UMHE"). Established in 1964, the United Ministries in Higher Education emerged out of the United Campus Christian Fellowship, an organization that represented the student Christian movements of four Protestant denominations. During the early-1970s, UMHE hosted meetings and events of the Lincoln Gay Action Group ("LINGAG") at its headquarters at 333 North 14th Street in Lincoln, NE.

"Employment of Homosexuals And Other Sex Perverts in Government" (1950). On December 15, 1950, the Subcommittee on Investigations of the US Senate Committee on Expenditures in the Executive Departments printed "Employment of Homosexuals And Other Sex Perverts in Government," which concluded that "those who engage in acts of homosexuality and other perverted-sex activities are unsuitable for employment in the Federal Government." The Senate authorized the investigation with the passage of Senate Resolution 280 (June 7, 1950), which was influenced by earlier investigations conducted by US Senator Kenneth S. Executive Order 10450. On April 27, 1953, President Dwight Eisenhower signed Executive Order 10450, which identified people with criminal records of "sexual perversion" as threats to national security and barred or terminated them from federal employment. In the 1950s, between 7,000 and 10,000 workers lost jobs due to suspicions of homosexuality. President Barack Obama revoked the entirety of the Eisenhower order through his Executive Order 13488, which he signed on January 17, 2017.

Nixon in Nebraska. President Richard Nixon visited Omaha, NE, for ninety minutes on October 29, 1970.

Stonewall Riots. The Stonewall Riots, often referred to as the "Stonewall Uprising" or simply "Stonewall" took place in the early morning hours of June 28, 1969, when New York City police officers raided the Stonewall Inn, a gay bar in Greenwich Village. At the time, New York State law prohibited public displays of homosexual behavior including holding hands, kissing, or dancing with someone of the same sex. The raid sparked outrage and led to six days of protests between officers and bar patrons. Stonewall is often credited as being the catalyst for the gay rights movement in the United States.

Vice Chancellor Hobson's Speech. Speaking on curriculum, Vice Chancellor Merk Hobson stated "those who know the subject best should decide the curriculum” and that the university was designed to "protect what is taught from the changing political scene." ("Current role of university elements outlined in Montgomery lecture." The Daily Nebraskan. 5 May 1969.)
